PERIPHERAL NERVE ANATOMY AND OCCUPATIONAL THERAPY CLINICAL SUPPLEMENT
University at Buffalo — OT Anatomy Reference

This supplement covers the three major peripheral nerves of the upper limb —
ulnar, radial, and median — with emphasis on clinical presentation, functional
consequences, and occupational therapy assessment and intervention.

================================================================================
SECTION 1: ULNAR NERVE — ANATOMY, CLINICAL PRESENTATION, AND OT RELEVANCE
================================================================================

Anatomy and Pathway
-------------------
The ulnar nerve arises from the medial cord of the brachial plexus and carries
fibers from nerve roots C8 and T1. It travels down the medial side of the arm,
passes posterior to the medial epicondyle of the humerus through the cubital
tunnel (the groove between the medial epicondyle and the olecranon), and enters
the forearm. The spot where the nerve passes behind the medial epicondyle is
commonly called the "funny bone" — bumping this area causes the characteristic
sharp tingling sensation that shoots down the forearm into the ring and little
fingers, because the nerve is compressed directly against bone.

In the forearm, the ulnar nerve innervates two muscles: the flexor carpi ulnaris
(wrist flexion toward the ulnar side) and the medial half of the flexor digitorum
profundus (deep flexion of the ring and little fingers). In the hand, the ulnar
nerve innervates most of the intrinsic hand muscles: all four dorsal interossei,
three palmar interossei, the hypothenar muscles (abductor digiti minimi, flexor
digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi), the medial two lumbricals, and the
adductor pollicis.

Sensory Distribution
--------------------
The ulnar nerve provides sensation to the little finger (fifth digit) and the
ulnar (medial) half of the ring finger (fourth digit), as well as the
corresponding palm and dorsal hand on the ulnar side. Patients with ulnar nerve
injury report numbness or tingling specifically in these two fingers. This
specific sensory distribution — ring and little fingers — is a clinical hallmark
that distinguishes ulnar nerve injury from other peripheral nerve injuries.

Clinical Presentation of Ulnar Nerve Injury
--------------------------------------------
Compression or injury to the ulnar nerve at the elbow (cubital tunnel syndrome)
or at the wrist (Guyon's canal syndrome) produces a recognizable clinical picture:

Sensory symptoms:
- Numbness and tingling in the ring finger and little finger
- Tingling sensation that shoots into the ring and little fingers when the
  medial epicondyle is bumped (positive Tinel's sign at the elbow)
- Reduced two-point discrimination on the ulnar side of the hand

Motor symptoms:
- Weakness of grip strength, particularly the power grip involving ring and
  little fingers
- Weakness when trying to spread the fingers apart (abduction) or bring them
  together (adduction), because the interossei are weak
- Difficulty pinching with the thumb and index finger due to weak adductor
  pollicis (Froment's sign: patient flexes the thumb IP joint to compensate)
- In severe or chronic cases: claw hand deformity, where the ring and little
  fingers hyperextend at the MCP joint and flex at the PIP and DIP joints
  (due to loss of intrinsic muscle balance)
- Wasting (atrophy) of the hypothenar eminence and interosseous muscles,
  visible as hollowing between the metacarpals on the dorsum of the hand

Functional Consequences for Daily Activities
--------------------------------------------
Ulnar nerve dysfunction significantly impairs fine motor tasks and power grip.
Patients struggle with activities of daily living (ADLs) such as:
- Buttoning a shirt or coat: requires coordinated ring and little finger
  movements and precise pinch that depend on ulnar intrinsic muscles
- Holding a key between the fingers: weakened grip and pinch reduce the force
  needed to turn a key
- Typing on a keyboard: ring and little finger weakness reduces speed and
  accuracy for the ulnar keys
- Using scissors: requires coordinated finger spreading and closing
- Picking up small objects: impaired fine pinch and reduced sensory feedback
  from the ulnar fingers
- Writing: reduced grip endurance and altered pen control
- Opening jars or bottles: reduced grip strength from intrinsic muscle weakness

Occupational Therapy Assessment
--------------------------------
OT assessment for ulnar nerve injury includes:
- Grip and pinch strength dynamometry (compare bilateral, document deficits)
- Semmes-Weinstein monofilament testing for sensory threshold in ulnar digits
- Two-point discrimination testing (ring and little fingers)
- Manual muscle testing: interossei, hypothenar muscles, flexor carpi ulnaris
- Froment's sign (paper pinch test for adductor pollicis weakness)
- Functional ADL observation: buttoning, key use, writing, object manipulation
- Patient-reported outcome measures (e.g., DASH, MHQ)

Occupational Therapy Intervention
-----------------------------------
OT interventions for ulnar nerve injury target function restoration and
compensation:
- Splinting: anti-claw splint to prevent MCP hyperextension and maintain
  functional hand position, especially for ring and little fingers
- Sensory re-education: graded tactile stimulation to restore sensory mapping
  in the ring and little fingers
- Strengthening: progressive resistive exercises for grip, pinch, and
  intrinsic hand muscles as nerve recovery allows
- Compensatory strategies: adapted techniques for buttoning, writing, and
  object manipulation to maintain independence in ADLs
- Nerve gliding exercises: ulnar nerve glides to reduce adhesion and improve
  nerve mobility through the cubital tunnel
- Education: positioning to avoid prolonged elbow flexion (which stretches
  the ulnar nerve at the cubital tunnel) and pressure on the medial elbow
- Work modification: ergonomic adjustments to reduce repetitive elbow flexion
  or pressure on the ulnar nerve

SECTION 2: RADIAL NERVE — ANATOMY, CLINICAL PRESENTATION, AND OT RELEVANCE
================================================================================

Anatomy and Pathway
-------------------
The radial nerve is the largest branch of the brachial plexus. It arises from
the posterior cord and carries fibers from nerve roots C5, C6, C7, C8, and T1.
The radial nerve travels posterior to the humerus, winding around the bone in
the spiral groove (also called the radial groove), then passes anteriorly to
reach the lateral elbow. It divides into the superficial radial nerve (sensory)
and the deep branch — also called the posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) — which
is purely motor and enters the posterior forearm through the supinator muscle.

The radial nerve innervates the triceps brachii (elbow extension), brachioradialis,
and all the extensor muscles of the wrist and fingers in the forearm: extensor
carpi radialis longus and brevis, extensor digitorum, extensor digiti minimi,
extensor carpi ulnaris, extensor pollicis longus and brevis, abductor pollicis
longus, and extensor indicis.

Sensory Distribution
--------------------
The radial nerve provides sensation to the posterior arm, posterior forearm, and
the dorsal surface of the lateral hand and the proximal dorsal surface of the
thumb, index, middle, and half of the ring finger. Unlike the ulnar nerve which
covers the little finger, the radial nerve covers the dorsal thumb and index finger.

Clinical Presentation of Radial Nerve Injury
----------------------------------------------
Radial nerve injury at the level of the spiral groove (common in humeral shaft
fractures or "Saturday night palsy" from prolonged compression of the arm) causes
the classic wrist drop deformity:

Motor symptoms:
- Wrist drop: inability to extend the wrist against gravity, because all wrist
  extensors are paralyzed
- Inability to extend the fingers at the MCP joints
- Inability to extend or abduct the thumb
- Weakness of supination (if injury is proximal to the supinator branch)
- Triceps weakness and absent elbow extension (if injury is proximal to the
  triceps branch — very proximal injuries only)

Sensory symptoms:
- Numbness or reduced sensation over the dorsal thumb web space and dorsal
  lateral hand (the area supplied by the superficial radial nerve)

Comparison with Ulnar Nerve
-----------------------------
The radial nerve and ulnar nerve have opposite functional roles in the hand:
- Radial nerve controls extension (opening the hand, extending the wrist)
- Ulnar nerve controls intrinsic hand muscles (spreading fingers, fine pinch,
  grip with ring and little fingers)
A patient with radial nerve injury cannot open the hand (wrist drop, no finger
extension), while a patient with ulnar nerve injury can open the hand but cannot
coordinate fine grip and pinch.

Functional Consequences for Daily Activities
--------------------------------------------
Radial nerve palsy (wrist drop) severely impairs upper limb function because
the wrist must be stabilized in extension for effective grasp:
- Picking up objects: without wrist extension, grasp power is dramatically reduced
- Self-care: dressing, grooming, and feeding all require wrist stability
- Writing: impossible with flail wrist
- Keyboard use: cannot position hand correctly for typing
- Reaching and placing objects: wrist instability limits all reach-and-grasp tasks

Occupational Therapy Intervention
-----------------------------------
- Cock-up wrist splint or dynamic wrist extension splint to substitute for lost
  wrist extension and restore functional hand position for grasp
- Tenodesis grasp training: using wrist flexion-extension to passively open and
  close the hand
- Compensatory ADL strategies: modified grip techniques
- Nerve gliding exercises for the radial nerve along the posterior arm
- Progressive strengthening as nerve recovers (radial nerve regeneration can
  take months given the long distance from spiral groove to wrist extensors)

SECTION 3: MEDIAN NERVE — ANATOMY, CLINICAL PRESENTATION, AND OT RELEVANCE
================================================================================

Anatomy and Pathway
-------------------
The median nerve arises from both the medial and lateral cords of the brachial
plexus (nerve roots C6, C7, C8, T1). It travels down the medial side of the arm
alongside the brachial artery, passes through the antecubital fossa, and enters
the forearm between the two heads of the pronator teres. It runs deep through
the forearm, innervating most flexor muscles, and enters the hand through the
carpal tunnel — the fibro-osseous tunnel at the wrist formed by the carpal bones
and the flexor retinaculum (transverse carpal ligament).

The median nerve innervates the forearm flexors (pronator teres, flexor carpi
radialis, palmaris longus, flexor digitorum superficialis, and the radial half of
flexor digitorum profundus), as well as the thenar muscles in the hand (abductor
pollicis brevis, opponens pollicis, and flexor pollicis brevis) and the first and
second lumbricals.

Sensory Distribution
--------------------
The median nerve provides sensation to the palmar surface of the thumb, index,
middle, and radial half of the ring finger — the most discriminative region of
the hand used for precision tasks. Loss of sensation here profoundly impairs
fine motor skills because these fingers are used for nearly all pinch and
manipulation tasks.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
----------------------
Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is the most common peripheral nerve compression in
the upper limb. Compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel at the
wrist causes:
- Numbness and tingling in the thumb, index, middle, and radial ring finger
- Nocturnal symptoms (patients wake with tingling, shake hand for relief)
- Weakness of thumb opposition (opponens pollicis)
- Weakness of thenar eminence with atrophy in chronic cases
- Difficulty with precision pinch tasks: sewing, buttoning, picking up small coins
- Positive Phalen's test (sustained wrist flexion reproduces symptoms) and
  positive Tinel's sign at the carpal tunnel

OT Intervention for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
--------------------------------------------
- Neutral wrist splint for night use (prevents wrist flexion that compresses nerve)
- Activity modification: reduce sustained wrist flexion, reduce vibration exposure
- Nerve gliding exercises (median nerve tendon glides)
- Thenar strengthening after surgical decompression
- Sensory re-education for the palmar thumb and fingers
- Ergonomic assessment of workstation and tools

SECTION 4: PERIPHERAL NERVE ASSESSMENT IN OCCUPATIONAL THERAPY PRACTICE
================================================================================

Screening and Assessment Tools
--------------------------------
Occupational therapists use the following when evaluating peripheral nerve injuries:

Sensation:
- Semmes-Weinstein monofilament testing: quantifies pressure threshold in each
  nerve distribution; 2.83 filament = normal, above 4.56 = protective sensation loss
- Two-point discrimination: static and moving 2PD assess functional sensory recovery
- Tinel's sign: percussion along the nerve course to identify the level of regeneration
- Phalen's test (median nerve), elbow flexion test (ulnar nerve): provocative testing

Motor:
- Manual muscle testing (MMT) for specific muscles in each nerve distribution
- Grip dynamometry (Jamar) and pinch gauge (lateral, three-jaw chuck, tip pinch)
- Observation of intrinsic minus hand position (claw) or extrinsic minus (wrist drop)

Function:
- Purdue Pegboard Test: assembly task requiring coordinated fine motor skill
- Jebsen-Taylor Hand Function Test: timed ADL-based tasks (writing, card turning,
  object manipulation, simulated feeding)
- DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand): patient-reported upper
  extremity disability
- Canadian Occupational Performance Measure (COPM): client-identified priority
  occupations affected by the nerve injury

Nerve Regeneration and Recovery
---------------------------------
Peripheral nerves regenerate at approximately 1 mm per day (roughly 1 inch per
month). Recovery after a peripheral nerve injury follows a predictable sequence:
- Pain and hypersensitivity resolve first
- Protective sensation (ability to detect sharp/dull) returns before discriminative
  sensation (two-point discrimination)
- Proximal muscles recover before distal muscles (the nerve must grow from the
  injury site down to the muscle)
- Full motor and sensory recovery may take months to years depending on injury
  severity and distance

OT focuses on maximizing functional independence throughout the recovery process:
- Providing adaptive equipment and splints during the paralytic phase
- Guiding sensory re-education as sensation returns
- Progressing strengthening as motor function recovers
- Modifying home and work environments to accommodate residual deficits

SECTION 5: BRACHIAL PLEXUS OVERVIEW AND CLINICAL RELEVANCE
================================================================================

The brachial plexus is the network of nerves arising from the ventral rami of
spinal nerves C5, C6, C7, C8, and T1. These five roots combine to form three
trunks (upper, middle, lower), which divide into anterior and posterior divisions,
which recombine to form three cords (lateral, posterior, medial), and finally
branch into the five major terminal nerves of the upper limb:
- Musculocutaneous nerve (C5, C6): elbow flexion, forearm sensation
- Median nerve (C6-T1): forearm flexors, thenar muscles, palmar sensation
- Ulnar nerve (C8, T1): intrinsic hand muscles, ring and little finger sensation
- Radial nerve (C5-T1): all extensors, dorsal arm/forearm/hand sensation
- Axillary nerve (C5, C6): deltoid and teres minor, shoulder sensation

Clinical Relevance for OT
--------------------------
Understanding the brachial plexus organization allows OTs to predict which
muscles and sensory areas will be affected based on the level of injury:
- Upper plexus injury (C5-C6, Erb's palsy): affects shoulder and elbow; arm
  hangs in internal rotation, elbow extended ("waiter's tip" posture)
- Lower plexus injury (C8-T1, Klumpke's palsy): affects hand intrinsics,
  finger flexors, and ulnar sensation; causes claw hand
- Complete plexus injury: flail arm with total anesthesia of the limb

OT assessment after brachial plexus injury maps motor and sensory deficits to
identify which nerve roots are injured, guides prognosis, and targets intervention
to maintain passive range of motion, prevent contracture, protect insensate skin,
and restore functional independence in self-care and work tasks.
